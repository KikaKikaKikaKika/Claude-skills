# Co-Design Workshop Template

Use for: generating ideas with users rather than for users, exploring directions in ideation phase, building alignment between users and stakeholders, situations where the problem space is ambiguous and you need diverse perspectives.
Not suitable for: validating whether a specific design works (use usability testing), large-scale quantitative insight (use surveys), situations where participants need to be kept blind to avoid bias.

---

## What it is

A co-design workshop invites users (and sometimes stakeholders) to participate in the design process — generating ideas, reacting to directions, and making decisions together. The output is not a final design; it's raw material: ideas, priorities, language, and insight that inform the design direction.

The designer's role shifts from creator to facilitator. Your job is to make participation easy, capture what emerges, and synthesise it into direction — not to present your own ideas for feedback.

## Workshop Design Principles

- **Start with their world, not your product.** Ground participants in their real context before introducing any design stimulus.
- **Low-fidelity stimulus only.** If you show polished designs, participants polish-critique instead of generate.
- **Equal voices.** Use techniques that prevent dominant voices from drowning out quieter ones (individual silent writing before group discussion).
- **Capture everything.** Don't filter in the room. Synthesise later.
- **Be honest about the purpose.** Tell participants their input is directional, not a commitment to build everything they suggest.

## Workshop Agenda Template

### Slot 1: Welcome and Warm-up (15 min)

**Purpose:** Set tone, build trust, establish ground rules.

> "Today we're going to work together to explore [problem space]. You're the experts on your own experience — we're here to learn from you. There are no wrong answers. We'll be asking you to share ideas and reactions, and everything will be captured."

**Ground rules (display throughout):**
- All ideas are valid — we filter later, not now
- One conversation at a time
- Build on each other's ideas
- There's no right answer

**Warm-up activity (5 min):** Choose one:
- "One word to describe [relevant experience]" — go around the room
- "Show us something from your phone that you use every day for [relevant activity]"
- Quick icebreaker relevant to the topic

---

### Slot 2: Context Setting — Their World (20 min)

**Purpose:** Activate participants' real experience before any design stimulus.

**Activity: Journey mapping or experience timeline**

Ask participants individually (then share):
- "Map out the last time you did [relevant activity]. What happened, step by step?"
- Prompt: what were you doing, thinking, feeling at each step?

Share back: each participant walks through their experience. Facilitator notes themes, tensions, and moments of friction on a shared board.

**Output:** Shared picture of the current experience — foundation for the rest of the workshop.

---

### Slot 3: Problem Framing (15 min)

**Purpose:** Agree on the problem before generating solutions.

**Activity: "How Might We" reframing**

Based on the journey map, generate HMW questions:
> "How might we [help users] [achieve goal] without [pain point]?"

Each participant generates 3–5 HMW questions on post-its. Cluster and vote on the most important ones. The top 1–2 HMW questions become the frame for the ideation phase.

---

### Slot 4: Ideation (30–40 min)

**Purpose:** Generate many ideas, withholding judgement.

**Activity: Sketching or card-based ideation**

Option A — Crazy 8s (individual, then share):
> "You have 8 minutes. Sketch 8 different ideas — one per box. Don't worry about quality. Quantity is the goal."

Option B — Idea cards (provide stimulus):
> Give participants cards with design patterns, analogies from other domains, or "what if" prompts. Ask them to generate ideas inspired by the cards.

Share back: each participant presents 1–2 favourite ideas. Group discusses and builds.

**Facilitator role:** write down every idea without filtering. Ask "how might that work?" when ideas are vague. Prevent criticism — redirect to "what's interesting about that idea?"

---

### Slot 5: Prioritisation (15–20 min)

**Purpose:** Move from many ideas to a few worth pursuing.

**Activity: Dot voting + brief discussion**

Each participant gets 3–5 votes (dots). Vote on ideas across the room — can put multiple votes on one idea.

After voting:
- "What does the voting tell us? What surprised you?"
- "For the top ideas — what would need to be true for this to work?"
- "What's missing?"

---

### Slot 6: Close and Next Steps (10 min)

> "We've covered a lot today. I want to share what we'll do with this."

- Thank participants explicitly
- Explain what happens next: "We'll synthesise what we heard today and share a summary within [X days]. This will directly inform [what comes next]."
- Collect feedback on the workshop itself (optional sticky note reflection: "What worked / what would you change?")

## Facilitator Notes

**Managing dominant voices:**
- Use individual silent writing before group sharing — it equalises contribution
- Direct questions to quieter participants: "We haven't heard from [name] yet — what's your take?"
- In remote workshops, use anonymous input (Miro voting, anonymous post-its) to reduce anchoring

**When discussion stalls:**
- Change the stimulus: show an example from a different domain
- Reframe the question: "What would [famous company] do here?"
- Ask "what would make this idea 10x better?" to unstick incremental thinking

**Remote workshop adaptations:**
- Use Miro, FigJam, or MURAL for shared boards
- Build in more time — everything takes longer remotely
- Use breakout rooms for small group activities
- Assign a co-facilitator specifically for tech support

---

## Analysis Notes

**Immediately after:**
- Take photos or export the board
- Write a quick summary: top themes, surprising ideas, moments of strong agreement or tension
- Note ideas that came up repeatedly — those have the most signal

**Synthesis:**
- Cluster ideas by theme
- Note the language participants used — these are often better labels than internal product terms
- Identify gaps: what did participants NOT generate that you expected?
- Prioritise ideas that scored highly AND connect to identified pain points

**What to present to stakeholders:**
- Top 3–5 directions that emerged, with the participant reasoning behind each
- Verbatim language from participants (especially for naming and copy)
- What problems participants confirmed as real vs what surprised you
- Recommended next step (usually: concept testing or prototype the top 1–2 directions)

**What co-design will NOT tell you:**
- Whether a specific design works (use usability testing)
- Whether users will actually use what they say they want (stated preference ≠ behaviour)
- Anything statistically representative (use surveys for that)
