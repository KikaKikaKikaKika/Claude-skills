# Guerrilla Testing Template

Use for: quick, cheap validation when you have limited time or budget, early-stage designs where directional feedback is enough, situations where recruiting formal participants isn't possible.
Not suitable for: specialised or hard-to-find user types, rigorous validation, research that needs to be credible to skeptical stakeholders, anything where context or domain expertise matters.

---

## What it is

Guerrilla testing means intercepting willing strangers (in a café, library, co-working space, or online community) to do quick, informal usability tests. Sessions are short (5–15 minutes), unscheduled, and low-cost. The trade-off: participants are not your target users, so findings are directional at best.

It's better than nothing. It's not better than proper usability testing with the right participants.

---

## When it's appropriate

- You have 1–2 days and no recruiting budget
- You're testing basic usability — navigation, comprehension, obvious confusion points
- You just need to know if something is obviously broken before a real test
- You need to convince a stakeholder that there's a problem before investing in research

When it's NOT appropriate:
- The product requires domain knowledge (medical, legal, financial, technical)
- You're testing B2B software with specialist workflows
- The finding needs to be statistically defensible
- The design relies heavily on user context or mental models

## Session Guide

### Approaching participants

> "Hi — I'm doing some quick research on a design I'm working on. It would take about 10 minutes. You'd be helping me a lot. Can I buy you a coffee?"

Accept any decline gracefully. Don't pitch further.

### Opening (1–2 min)

> "I'm going to show you something I'm working on and ask you to try a couple of things. I'm testing the design, not you — there are no right or wrong answers. Please think out loud as you go. Any questions before we start?"

### Tasks (5–10 min)

Keep it to 1–3 tasks. Write them on a card so the participant can refer back.

Each task should:
- Be written as a realistic goal, not an instruction
- Be completable in 2–4 minutes
- Have a clear success state

Example:
> "Imagine you want to invite a colleague to your account. Go ahead and try to do that."

Watch for: hesitation, wrong paths, confusion, error recovery. Take brief notes.

### Close (1–2 min)

- "What was your overall impression?"
- "Was anything confusing?"
- "Is there anything you'd change?"

Thank them. Give incentive if you have one.

---

## Analysis Notes

After 5–8 sessions:
- Note issues that came up in 3+ sessions — these are real problems
- Single-participant issues may be noise — flag but don't prioritise without more data
- Pay attention to failure points, not just opinions

**What to tell stakeholders:**
- Lead with specific observations: "4 out of 7 people couldn't find the [X] button without help"
- Acknowledge the limitation: "These were not our target users — this is directional. We recommend following up with 5 recruited participants to confirm."

**What guerrilla testing will NOT tell you:**
- How your actual users experience the product
- Whether domain-specific knowledge affects the experience
- Anything statistically reliable
