# Diary Study Template

Use for: understanding behaviour over time, capturing experiences that are hard to observe in a lab (infrequent events, private contexts, longitudinal changes), post-launch research on how usage evolves.
Not suitable for: quick turnaround (minimum 1–2 weeks), validating a specific design, situations where you can recruit and schedule easily (use contextual inquiry instead).

---

## What it is

A diary study asks participants to record their own experiences as they happen — over days or weeks — using prompted or unprompted entries. It captures what people actually do and feel in the moment, rather than what they remember in an interview.

Two types:
- **Prompted**: you send signals (daily push notifications, texts, or emails) asking participants to log at specific times or after specific events
- **Unprompted / event-contingent**: participants log whenever a specific event occurs ("log every time you [do X]")

Use prompted for understanding routine behaviour. Use event-contingent for studying specific triggers or infrequent events.

## Recruitment & Briefing

Diary studies have high dropout. Over-recruit by 30%.

**Screening criteria:**
- [Who they are / what they need to be doing during the study period]
- Willing to log for [X] days
- Has access to [smartphone / computer] during the study period
- Not travelling or on leave during the study window

**Briefing session (30 min, before the study starts):**
> "Before the study starts, I want to walk you through exactly what I'm asking you to do, show you the tool, and answer any questions. The most important thing is consistency — even a short entry is better than a missed one."

- Walk through the tool and show them how to submit an entry
- Show example entries (good and bad)
- Confirm the prompting schedule and what the event trigger is (if event-contingent)
- Confirm incentive structure and payment timing

## Entry Prompts

Design prompts to be quick to answer and open enough to capture real experience. Avoid yes/no questions.

### Prompted study (daily or timed) — example prompt set:

Each entry should take 5–10 minutes. Send 3–5 questions max per prompt.

1. "What were you doing when this prompt arrived?" [Short text]
2. "Describe the last time today you [relevant activity]. What happened?" [Open text]
3. "How did that go?" [Rating 1–5 + optional comment]
4. "Did anything frustrate or surprise you?" [Open text]
5. "Photo or screenshot (optional):" [Media upload]

### Event-contingent study — example prompt set:

Triggered when participant [does X]:

1. "What just happened? Briefly describe the situation." [Open text]
2. "What were you trying to do?" [Open text]
3. "What did you actually do?" [Open text]
4. "How did it make you feel?" [Emotion scale or open text]
5. "Capture a photo or screenshot if relevant:" [Media upload — optional]

## Researcher Activities During the Study

Don't disappear once the study starts.

**Daily:**
- Review new entries — flag missing participants before they slip further
- Note emerging themes while they're fresh
- Send a brief acknowledgement to participants (shows you're reading and engaged)

**Mid-study check-in (day 3–5):**
- Brief message or call to each participant: "How's it going? Any questions?"
- Identify participants who are struggling — prompt or adjust before they drop out

**If entries are too short or vague:**
> "Thanks for your entry yesterday. I wanted to follow up on one thing — when you said [X], can you tell me a bit more about what that looked like?"

---

## Close: Exit Interview (30–45 min)

Run a brief interview at the end of the study. The diary is the data; the interview helps you interpret it.

- "Looking back over the study period, what stands out as the most significant moment?"
- "Were there things you wanted to log but couldn't or didn't?"
- "Did anything change in how you [did X] over the course of the study?"
- "What would you want us to know that your entries didn't capture?"

---

## Analysis Notes

**Organise entries by:**
- Participant over time (longitudinal view — how did their experience change?)
- Theme across participants (cross-sectional view — what patterns appear?)
- Incident type (for event-contingent — what triggered entries, how were they resolved?)

**What to look for:**
- Behaviour change over time (do people adapt, develop habits, give up?)
- Gap between early and late entries (novelty effect wearing off)
- High-emotion moments — these are signal worth exploring in the exit interview
- Artefacts (photos, screenshots) that reveal context you couldn't have observed

**Attrition:**
- 15–30% dropout is normal. Plan for it.
- Exclude participants with fewer than 50% of expected entries from primary analysis

**What diary studies will NOT tell you:**
- Whether a design works for a task (use usability testing)
- How representative findings are at scale (use surveys)
- The "why" behind a behaviour in depth (combine with interviews or contextual inquiry)
