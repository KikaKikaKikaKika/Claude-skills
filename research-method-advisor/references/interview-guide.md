# Interview Guide Template

Use for: generative user interviews, stakeholder interviews, discovery research.
Not suitable for: validating whether a design works — use usability-test-plan.md instead.

---

## Structure

A good interview guide has five parts: warm-up, context, core questions, concept reaction (optional), and close. Total time: 45–60 minutes. Never exceed 90 minutes.

---

---

## Moderator Notes

- This is a guide, not a script. Follow the conversation.
- Your job is to listen, not to explain or sell.
- Never ask leading questions ("Do you find this confusing?"). Ask open ones ("How would you describe your experience with this?").
- Silence is useful. Wait it out before prompting.
- When someone gives a short answer, probe: "Tell me more about that." / "What did you mean by [word]?" / "Can you give me an example?"
- Never correct the participant. If they misunderstand something, note it — that's signal.

---

## Part 1: Warm-up (5 min)

Goal: build rapport, confirm consent, set expectations.

> "Thanks for making time. Before we start, a few things — this is about understanding your experience, not testing you. There are no right or wrong answers. I may ask you to explain things in detail, or to think out loud. And with your permission, I'd like to record this so I can focus on the conversation. Is that OK?"

- Tell me a bit about yourself and your role.
- How long have you been doing [relevant activity]?
- What does a typical [day / week] look like for you?

---

## Part 2: Context (10 min)

Goal: understand the user's world before getting to the specific topic.

Adapt these to the research topic — replace [topic] with the specific domain:

- Walk me through the last time you [did the relevant task or behaviour].
- What were you trying to accomplish?
- What tools or resources did you use?
- Where did things go well? Where did things feel harder than they should?
- What do you do when [common friction point] happens?

---

## Part 3: Core Questions (25–30 min)

Goal: explore the specific research questions in depth.

Write 4–6 core questions here, tailored to the research topic. Guidelines:
- Start broad, go narrow.
- One question at a time.
- Use "walk me through" and "tell me about a time when" to get stories, not opinions.
- Avoid hypotheticals ("Would you use...?") — they produce unreliable data. Ask about past behaviour instead.

Example structure:
1. "Tell me about the last time you [did X]. What happened?"
2. "What made that [easy / hard / frustrating]?"
3. "How do you currently handle [pain point]?"
4. "What have you tried before? What happened?"
5. "If you could change one thing about [process / tool / experience], what would it be?"
6. "Is there anything about [topic] we haven't talked about that feels important?"

---

## Part 4: Concept Reaction (optional — 10 min)

Use only if you have early concepts, sketches, or prototypes to react to. Do not use to validate — use a usability test for that.

> "I want to show you something early — this is just an idea, not a finished product. I'm interested in your first reaction, not whether you like it."

- What's your first impression?
- What does this remind you of?
- What questions come up for you?
- What's missing or unclear?

---

## Part 5: Close (5 min)

- Is there anything you expected me to ask that I didn't?
- Is there anyone else you think I should talk to?
- Any questions for me?

> "That's everything from me. Thank you — this has been really useful. [Explain next steps / incentive if applicable.]"

---

## After the Interview: Analysis Notes

**What to capture immediately (within 1 hour):**
- Top 3 things you heard that surprised you
- Direct quotes worth preserving verbatim
- Moments of strong emotion (frustration, delight, confusion)
- Behaviours or workarounds you hadn't anticipated

**Synthesis approach:**
- Affinity mapping: cluster notes by theme across participants
- Look for patterns in: goals, pain points, mental models, workarounds
- Note frequency (how many said it) and intensity (how strongly they felt it) separately
- 5–8 participants is usually enough to identify major themes

**What interviews will NOT tell you:**
- Whether users can complete a task (use usability testing)
- Which version of a design performs better (use A/B testing)
- How representative the findings are at scale (use a survey)
