# Concept Testing Template

Use for: getting early reactions to ideas before investing in detailed design, testing multiple directions at once, understanding which direction resonates with users and why.
Not suitable for: validating whether a design works (use usability testing), understanding user behaviour in context (use contextual inquiry or interviews).

---

## What it is

Concept testing exposes users to early-stage ideas — sketches, storyboards, mood boards, or rough prototypes — and captures their reactions. The goal is directional signal: does this resonate, does it solve the right problem, which direction is worth pursuing?

Key principle: **concepts should be rough enough that participants respond to the idea, not the execution.** If you show polished designs, feedback shifts to visual details. Keep it lo-fi.

## Preparing Concepts

**What works for concept testing:**
- Hand-drawn sketches or low-fidelity wireframes
- Storyboards showing the user scenario and the solution
- Value proposition statements ("This product helps [user] to [goal] by [mechanism]")
- Mood boards for visual/brand direction
- Rough clickable prototypes (Figma, paper prototype)

**What doesn't work:**
- Polished high-fidelity screens — participants will focus on colour, font, and details
- Only one concept — you learn more from comparison
- Concepts that aren't distinct enough — ensure they represent genuinely different directions

**Number of concepts:** 2–4 is optimal. More than 4 causes fatigue and shallow feedback on each.

**Counterbalance presentation order** across participants to remove primacy bias.

## Session Guide

### Opening (5 min)

> "Today I'm going to show you some early ideas — these are rough on purpose. I want your honest reactions, not polish feedback. There's no right answer. I'm more interested in whether these ideas feel relevant to your life than whether you like how they look."

- Brief warm-up: "Tell me about how you currently [do the relevant thing]." (2–3 minutes — activates their mental context before you show concepts)

---

### Concept Presentation — Per Concept (10 min each)

**Step 1: Silent review**
> "Take a look at this for a moment. Don't say anything yet — just read through it."
(Give 60–90 seconds of silence.)

**Step 2: First impression**
- "What's your first reaction?"
- "What does this remind you of?"
- "What do you think this is / does?"

**Step 3: Relevance and resonance**
- "Does this feel like something that would be useful to you? Why or why not?"
- "Who do you think this is designed for?"
- "What would have to be true for this to be part of your life / workflow?"

**Step 4: Concerns and gaps**
- "What concerns or questions does this raise for you?"
- "What's missing?"
- "What would make this feel more (or less) trustworthy?"

---

### Comparison (10 min — after showing all concepts)

Once all concepts have been seen individually:

- "Now that you've seen all of them — which direction resonates most with you? Why?"
- "Which feels least relevant to your situation?"
- "If you had to combine the best parts of these, what would you keep from each?"
- "Is there a direction we haven't shown you that you think would work better?"

---

### Close (5 min)

- "Is there anything important about [topic] that none of these addressed?"
- "Anything you want to add before we finish?"

## Analysis Notes

**Per concept, track:**
- First impression (positive / neutral / negative)
- Perceived relevance (would / wouldn't use)
- Key concerns raised
- Unexpected interpretations (how they understood it vs how you intended it)

**Across participants:**
- Which concept generated the most positive first impressions?
- Which concept was most consistently understood as intended?
- Which concerns appeared across multiple participants?
- Were there interpretations or use cases you hadn't anticipated?

**Decision framework:**
Concept testing is directional, not definitive. Use it to:
- Eliminate directions that clearly don't resonate
- Identify which direction to invest in next
- Capture specific features or language from the winning concept

**What to present to stakeholders:**
- A clear recommendation: which direction to pursue and why
- The top 3 concerns to address in the next design iteration
- Verbatim quotes that illustrate the reaction to each concept
- What was learned that wasn't expected

**What concept testing will NOT tell you:**
- Whether users can use the product (use usability testing)
- Whether users will actually behave differently once they have it (stated preference ≠ behaviour)
- Which version performs better at scale (use A/B testing post-launch)
