# Contextual Inquiry Template

Use for: understanding how users behave in their real environment, uncovering workarounds and tacit knowledge, discovery research where you don't yet know what the real problems are.
Not suitable for: validating a specific design, reaching a large sample, research that requires a controlled environment.

---

## What it is

Contextual inquiry is a field research method where you observe and interview users in their actual environment while they do real work. You're a guest, not an authority — the user is the expert. You watch, ask questions as things happen, and build a picture of their world that no interview or survey could give you.

The four principles:
1. **Context** — observe in the actual place the work happens (desk, shop floor, kitchen, etc.)
2. **Partnership** — work alongside, not as evaluator. "Help me understand your world."
3. **Interpretation** — share your interpretations as you go. "It sounds like you're doing X because Y — is that right?"
4. **Focus** — have a research focus, but stay open to what surprises you.

## Session Guide

### Before you arrive
- Confirm logistics: where to meet, what they'll be doing, who else will be present
- Prepare your focus questions — what are you most curious about?
- Bring: consent form, audio recorder, notebook, camera (if permitted)

### Opening (10 min)

> "Thanks for letting me come and see how you work. I want to be clear — I'm here to learn from you, not to evaluate anything you're doing. There's no right or wrong way. I'll mostly watch and ask questions as things come up. Please just go about your normal work as much as you can. Is it OK if I take notes / record?"

- "Before we start, can you briefly describe what you're working on today and what your main goals are for this session?"

### During observation — facilitator approach

Let them work. Don't interrupt flow. When they pause, probe:

**Interpretation checks (share your reading, invite correction):**
- "It looks like you did X — can you tell me why?"
- "I noticed you switched to [tool] there — what made you do that?"
- "You seemed to hesitate before doing that — what were you thinking?"

**Probing for tacit knowledge:**
- "You did that really quickly — is that something you've developed a routine for?"
- "How did you know to do it that way?"
- "What would happen if you didn't do that step?"

**Uncovering workarounds:**
- "I see you're using [spreadsheet / sticky notes / third-party tool] — tell me about that."
- "Is this how you're supposed to do it, or have you found your own way?"

**When something unexpected happens:**
- "That seemed unexpected — what just happened?"
- "How often does this come up?"
- "What do you usually do when this happens?"

### Artefacts (capture everything)
Ask to photograph or copy:
- Spreadsheets, documents, sticky notes, whiteboards they use
- Printed instructions or guides they've made themselves
- Workarounds and hacks they've built

Always ask permission before photographing. These artefacts often reveal more than the conversation.

### Close (15 min)

- "We're nearly out of time. Is there anything important about your work that we didn't get to see today?"
- "If you could change one thing about [task / tool / process], what would it be?"
- "Is there anyone else you'd recommend I observe who works differently to you?"

## After the Session: Analysis Notes

**Immediately after (within 1 hour):**
- Write up your top 5 observations while they're fresh
- Note any artefacts you captured and what they reveal
- Flag any moments of surprise or confusion

**Synthesis across participants:**
- Create a work model: map the sequence of tasks, tools used, and people involved
- Identify breakdowns: where do things go wrong, where do workarounds appear?
- Look for patterns across environments: what's consistent, what varies?
- Build an affinity diagram: cluster observations by theme

**What contextual inquiry will NOT tell you:**
- Whether a specific design solution works (use usability testing)
- How common a behaviour is across your user base (use surveys)
- What users want in the abstract (they often can't articulate it — observe instead)
